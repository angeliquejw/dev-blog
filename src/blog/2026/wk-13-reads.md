Generally, I do a lot of reading -- books, yes, but also blog posts, articles, newsletters, the like. And, lately, I've been doing some thinking about what I intake, what I do with it, and how to develop some reinforcing habits so my relationship to what I read isn't fleeting.

For books, I already do a fair amount of highlighting and notetaking. I capture and review those using [Readwise](https://readwise.io/), but haven't really been doing the same for non-book material. This is, to be clear, a me limitation and not a product one: Readwise does handle articles and a lot of other content you might throw its way, I just haven't been using it. My last article highlights in the app are from 2022.<sup id="return-fn1"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup>

In order to be more thoughtful about and engaged with what I read, I plan on doing more highlighting and notetaking with Readwise so I can revisit some of my takeaways and thoughts and  _may_ also start sharing some of that here, serving to both energize the blog a bit and to force me to write more about what I'm reading (and thinking).<sup id="return-fn2"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup> This post is a trial of those things (so please let me know if you find this _and_ find it interesting). ## 📄 [In between: An exploration of liminal space](https://stephmifsud.substack.com/p/in-between)

> It’s where you reflect on what the last chapter taught you. Where you set down what you no longer need to carry. Where, like the caterpillar, your imaginal cells are quietly doing their work — holding the shape of what’s coming, even when you can’t see it. Unlike the butterfly’s one time transformation, life offers us multiple opportunities to evolve through change. At each liminal stop, there is opportunity for more of our human blueprint to emerge. But only when we postpone the rush into the busyness of the next seemingly obvious course.

> So if you’re in a transition right now — in any part of your life — this is an invitation to stay with it a little longer and listen to what it has to teach you.
<cite>--Steph Mifsud</cite>

This piece spoke to me because I'm in my own liminal space between jobs, and I'm sharing it in part because being in that space isn't so unique right now. And, if that is something we share in common, you may also be seeing and taking in a lot of messages about how to search for a job in 2026, how your job is changing, whether your job or even industry will exist in the near future. If that feels familiar, I encourage you to read Steph's piece; it's a balm, but also a call to action.

Steph writes that the word *liminal* is based on a word that means *threshold*, which makes me think of the threshold of my own home. Entering the door into my home, there's a place to put things down: my bag, my keys, my shoes. Crossing in the other direction, there's the comfort of my porch and then the wide, wide world full of possibilities. I'm reminded, reading her words, that crossing one way and setting things down doesn't preclude another later journey back into the world.

## 📄 [Change has a burn rate too: The human cost hiding inside your transformation roadmap](https://rlsutter.substack.com/p/change-has-a-burn-rate-too)

> Research on organizational identity change shows that when people anticipate disruption to their organization’s identity, they experience elevated job anxiety and reduced affective commitment. That’s not about the quality of the change. It’s about the pace. The anxiety isn’t “this is a bad idea.” The anxiety is “I just finished adapting to the last idea and I don’t have the reserves to do it again.”

> I talked to a senior engineering manager last year who’d been through three reorgs in two years. Good ones, he said. Each one made structural sense. But he told me he’d stopped building relationships with new cross-functional partners because “what’s the point? They’ll move us again in six months.” He wasn’t cynical by temperament. He was conserving energy. The system had taught him that relational investment was a depreciating asset.
<cite>--Rebecca Sutter</cite>

Change management is one of those things that I've only learned about through experience -- as the person enduring change and also leading it. From both perspectives, I really appreciate what Rebecca has described in this post. Highly recommend reading this piece if you're in a position to make or coordinate organization/team transitions and shifts, as Rebecca has outlined some likely impacts to be thoughtful about and some guidelines for less chaotic and harmful org/team changes. She also points out that there's always a cost to org/team changes, even changes that folx agree are good and/or needed. The handful of people I've already shared this with felt as if the writing was directly, painfully describing their workplaces and teams; to me, that's a sign that this area needs a lot more care and attention from those directing changes.

## ▶️ [Coaching Desert](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tVTBB5bC4Vw)

> Your CEO in most startups is not likely to give you any meaningful coaching. Some say that they're lucky if the CEO even gives the good, decent feedback.
<cite>--Aviv Ben-Yosef</cite>

Not an article, but this is a short and sweet video that's worthwhile if you're currently in a director-level or above position (or are targeting those sort of leadership roles). Aviv, first, acknowledges that at a certain level of leadership you unfortunately can't expect to get the meaningful, actionable feedback that helps you improve or align to your boss's expectations. He then gives you three tips of how to take control and still get the feedback you need to do right and do well.

Bonus for me and my still new notetaking process: Readwise totally handles additions from YouTube transcripts.

## 📗 [Ambition Monster: A Memoir](https://app.thestorygraph.com/books/1493b59b-207e-499e-9afe-aa4ff5853977)

> When we talk about burnout, we most often discuss feelings of overwhelm; the physical, emotional, and mental exhaustion that occurs after periods of overwork and excessive stress, how we become numb, unable to achieve or even dream of what future achievements could be.

> But we less often talk about burnout in terms of grief, the grief for what you thought your career could be, what you thought you could be, the grief for the time you'll never get back, the grief for having given yourself over to something unworthy of you, the grief that springs from cruelty and mishandling, of never being given as good as you gave.
<cite>--Jennifer Romolini</cite>

This was a cathartic, validating, powerful read about one woman's relationship to work that felt all-too-familiar on so many fronts, even though my career has a very different shape than the author's. I finished the book and immediately started sharing quotes (like the one above) with my friends. I'm still finding my own words about burnout and career grief, so it was a kind of comfort to find my way through part of the experience through Jenn's words.
